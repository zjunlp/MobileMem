## Task
Generate {question_count} questions of type `{question_type}`.

## Question-Format Ratio Constraints
- These are hard target ratios for each question type within the final dataset:
  - `single_hop`: about 30% `multiple_choice`
  - `multi_hop`: about 15% `multiple_choice`
  - `knowledge_update`: about 30% `multiple_choice`
  - `temporal_reasoning`: about 20% `multiple_choice`
  - `implicit_preference`: about 10% `multiple_choice`
- For the current `{question_type}` batch, choose `question_format` with this target ratio in mind rather than defaulting almost always to `open_ended`.
- If the current question type has a nonzero target ratio for `multiple_choice`, actively generate some valid `multiple_choice` questions instead of avoiding them.
- Keep all other constraints unchanged. This section only changes the target proportion of `multiple_choice` versus `open_ended`.

## Global Style Rules
- Write natural user-facing questions, not dataset labels or evaluator wording.
- The target persona is the user. If the user is the actor in the question, use first person such as `I`, `my`, `we`, or `our`.
- Do not refer to the target persona as `you`, `he`, `she`, or `they`.
- Keep stems short, direct, and grounded in realistic memory-assistant use cases.
- Internally divide the batch into two hidden length buckets for planning only. Do not output these bucket names or mention them anywhere in the JSON.
- Enforce a hard short-to-long ratio of 1:3 within each batch.
- If `{question_count}` is divisible by 4, exactly one quarter of the questions must be short and exactly three quarters must be long.
- If `{question_count}` is not divisible by 4, keep the short count as close as possible to one quarter, and never let the difference exceed 1 question from the exact 1:3 target.
- Short questions should sound like everyday chat and stay around 8-24 English words.
- Long questions must still stay spoken and natural, not analytical, and stay around 14-38 English words.
- Do not let most questions drift into the middle zone between the two target ranges. The short/long difference should be visible.
- Short questions may contain at most one short anchor phrase.
- Long questions may contain at most one situational anchor plus one brief follow-up clause. Do not let them turn into long narrative setup.
- Do not make every question follow the same template length or rhythm.
- If a situational anchor is needed, include a broad one such as a month, holiday period, trip, city, or event name.
- Do not use exact dates or exact timestamps in the question text.
- Do not leak the answer in the stem.
- Every answer must be grounded in the supplied context and supported by `evidence`.
- Forbidden long-form tendencies: do not first retell multiple events and then ask the real question; do not use summary framing such as `what this shows`, `taken together`, `from these events`, `reflects`, or similar evaluator-style wording.

## Question-Type Rules

### single_hop
- Center the question on one event or one situation, but require at least light reasoning, comparison, paraphrase normalization, or aggregation of nearby details.
- Do not write a pure one-fact lookup question.

### multi_hop
- Require combining 2-3 clues across dialogue and/or memory.
- Include some relationship and social-circle questions.
- Prefer asking which person fits a combined role/event description, how two people are connected, or which person is implied by multiple clues.
- Ask the question directly. Do not narrate the reasoning path in the stem.

### knowledge_update
- Ask about the latest or current state after a change.
- Prefer facts supported by `is_update=true` memory points.

### temporal_reasoning
- Ask about elapsed time, interval, duration, before/after, or timeline comparison.
- Prefer interval and duration questions over simple earliest/latest ranking.
- Do not include enough exact dates or timestamps in the question to make the answer mechanically solvable from the stem alone.

### implicit_preference
- Infer a likely choice or preference from at least two clues from profile, dialogue, plans, routines, spending, or prior decisions.
- Frame the question as a natural advice-seeking request from the user to the assistant.
- Example style: `I’m dealing with [specific situation]. What would you recommend I do?`
- Do not ask detached evaluator-style questions such as `What would he choose?`
- In the question text, `you` may refer only to the assistant giving advice, never to the target persona.

## Question Format
- Only use `open_ended` or `multiple_choice`.
- Do not generate `true_false`.
- If `question_format` is `multiple_choice`, provide exactly six options labeled `A. ...` through `F. ...`.
- For `multiple_choice`, `answer` must exactly match one full option string.
- For `open_ended`, keep the answer concise and do not include reasoning steps.

## Hard Constraints
- Do not include any type label, annotation prefix, or metadata wording in `question`, `answer`, or `options`.
- Do not generate questions answerable by copying one sentence from one memory point or one dialogue turn.
- Every question must require at least one of: combining multiple facts, aggregating details, temporal reasoning, latest-state reasoning, or multi-clue preference inference.
- Prefer comparison, attribution, integration, or constraint/tradeoff reasoning.
- Use the supplied dialogue and memory as the source of truth. Do not invent facts.
- If a question depends on a date, holiday, location, trip, or event anchor from the context, include that anchor in a broad form in the question.
- Use broad time expressions like `early this year`, `around the Spring Festival period`, `in the first half of the year`, or `during that weekend`, not exact dates.
- The stem must not directly state the correct conclusion, restate the correct option, or reveal uniquely identifying answer-bearing details.
- For `multiple_choice`, keep the stem especially lean and neutral.
- For `multiple_choice`, all six options must be balanced in length, detail, plausibility, and abstraction level.
- The correct option must not be obviously the longest, shortest, most polished, or only concrete option.
- Distractors must be near-neighbor alternatives in the same decision space.
